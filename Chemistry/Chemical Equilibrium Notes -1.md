# Chemical Equilibrium Notes -1

---

# 1. What is Chemical Equilibrium?

Imagine a **two-way road** between two cities.

Cars go:
City A → City B  
City B → City A

At first more cars may go from A to B.  
But after some time, **cars going both directions become equal**.

Cars are still moving, but the **number of cars in each city stops changing**.

Chemical equilibrium works the same way.

Molecules continue reacting, but the **overall amounts stop changing**.

---

## Definition

Chemical equilibrium is the **dynamic state of a reversible reaction** where:

$$
\text{Rate of forward reaction} = \text{Rate of backward reaction}
$$

Because of this:

- Concentration of reactants becomes constant  
- Concentration of products becomes constant  

Example reaction:

$$
N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)
$$

Left side → Reactants  
Right side → Products

The arrow $ \rightleftharpoons $

 means the reaction happens **in both directions**.

---

# 2. Reversible and Irreversible Reactions

## Irreversible Reaction

Reaction happens **only in one direction**.

Example:

$$
C(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g)
$$

Once carbon dioxide forms, it does **not easily go back** to carbon and oxygen.

---

## Reversible Reaction

Reaction happens in **both directions**.

Example:

$$
H_2(g) + I_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2HI(g)
$$

Here:

Forward reaction:

$$
H_2 + I_2 \rightarrow 2HI
$$

Backward reaction:

$$
2HI \rightarrow H_2 + I_2
$$

This type of reaction can reach **equilibrium**.

---

# 3. How Equilibrium is Achieved

Consider the reaction:

$$
N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)
$$

### Step 1: Beginning of Reaction

Lots of reactants  
No products

Forward reaction is **fast**.

Backward reaction is **zero**.

---

### Step 2: Products Start Forming

Ammonia begins forming.

Backward reaction **starts**.

---

### Step 3: After Some Time

Forward reaction slows down.  
Backward reaction speeds up.

Eventually:

$$
\text{Rate}_{forward} = \text{Rate}_{backward}
$$

Now equilibrium is reached.

---

# 4. Important Characteristics of Chemical Equilibrium

These are **very important exam points**.

### 1. Dynamic Nature

Even at equilibrium reactions continue.

Example:

$$
H_2 + I_2 \rightleftharpoons 2HI
$$

HI molecules keep forming and breaking.

But **overall concentration stays constant**.

---

### 2. Closed System Required

Equilibrium can only happen in a **closed container**.

If gas escapes:

Equilibrium is disturbed.

---

### 3. Concentration Becomes Constant

Example:

$$
[N_2] = 1.0 \, M
$$

$$
[H_2] = 3.0 \, M
$$

$$
[NH_3] = 0.5 \, M
$$

Values stay constant at equilibrium.

They **do not have to be equal**.

---

### 4. Equilibrium Can Be Reached From Both Directions

Starting with reactants:

$$
N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow NH_3
$$

Starting with product:

$$
NH_3 \rightarrow N_2 + H_2
$$

Both lead to the **same equilibrium state**.

---

### 5. Macroscopic Properties Remain Constant

Properties like:

- Pressure
- Colour
- Concentration

Remain constant at equilibrium.

---

# 5. Types of Equilibrium

Two main types exist.

1. Physical equilibrium  
2. Chemical equilibrium  

---

# 6. Physical Equilibrium

This occurs in **physical changes**, not chemical reactions.

Substance remains the same.

Example:

$$
H_2O(s) \rightleftharpoons H_2O(l)
$$

Both are water.

---

## Types of Physical Equilibrium

### Solid–Liquid Equilibrium

Example:

$$
H_2O(s) \rightleftharpoons H_2O(l)
$$

At melting point:

$$
\text{Rate of melting} = \text{Rate of freezing}
$$

---

### Liquid–Vapour Equilibrium

Example:

$$
H_2O(l) \rightleftharpoons H_2O(g)
$$

Evaporation = Condensation.

---

### Solid–Vapour Equilibrium

Example:

$$
I_2(s) \rightleftharpoons I_2(g)
$$

Iodine directly converts from solid to gas.

This is called **sublimation**.

---

# 7. Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium occurs in **chemical reactions**.

Example:

$$
H_2(g) + I_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2HI(g)
$$

New molecules are formed.

---

## Types of Chemical Equilibrium

### Homogeneous Equilibrium

All substances are in the **same phase**.

Example:

$$
N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)
$$

All are gases.

Another example:

$$
2SO_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3(g)
$$

---

### Heterogeneous Equilibrium

Substances are in **different phases**.

Example:

$$
CaCO_3(s) \rightleftharpoons CaO(s) + CO_2(g)
$$

Solid + solid + gas.

---

Important rule:

**Pure solids and liquids are not written in equilibrium expressions.**

---

# 8. Law of Mass Action

Proposed by:

**Guldberg and Waage (1864)**.

This law explains how **reaction rate depends on concentration**.

---

## Statement

The rate of a chemical reaction is proportional to the product of the **active masses (concentrations)** of the reactants.

Each concentration is raised to the power of its **stoichiometric coefficient**.

---

Example reaction:

$$
aA + bB \rightarrow Products
$$

Rate of reaction:

$$
Rate \propto [A]^a [B]^b
$$

Where:

$$
[A] = \text{concentration of A}
$$

$$
[B] = \text{concentration of B}
$$

---

Example:

$$
2NO(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2NO_2(g)
$$

Rate expression:

$$
Rate \propto [NO]^2 [O_2]
$$

---

# 9. Active Mass

Active mass means **effective concentration of a substance**.

Different forms:

Gas → partial pressure or concentration  
Solution → molar concentration  
Pure solid → constant  
Pure liquid → constant  

Because solids and liquids remain constant, they **do not appear in equilibrium expressions**.

---

Example:

$$
CaCO_3(s) \rightleftharpoons CaO(s) + CO_2(g)
$$

Only gas appears:

$$
K_c = [CO_2]
$$

---

# 10. Law of Chemical Equilibrium

This law comes from applying **law of mass action** to reversible reactions.

Consider reaction:

$$
aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD
$$

At equilibrium:

$$
K_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}
$$

Where:

- $K_c$ = equilibrium constant  
- Concentrations are taken **at equilibrium**

This value remains **constant at constant temperature**.

---

# 11. Equilibrium Constant (K)

When a reversible reaction reaches equilibrium, the **ratio of product concentration to reactant concentration becomes constant**.

For a general reaction:

$$
aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD
$$

The equilibrium constant in terms of concentration is:

$$
K_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}
$$

Where:

- $[A], [B], [C], [D]$ = equilibrium concentrations
- $a, b, c, d$ = stoichiometric coefficients

Important rule:

Always use **equilibrium concentrations**, not initial concentrations.

---

# 12. Understanding Equilibrium Constant Intuitively

Imagine two containers:

Left container → Reactants  
Right container → Products

Particles move between them continuously.

At equilibrium, the **ratio of particles on both sides becomes fixed**.

That fixed ratio is the **equilibrium constant (K)**.

---

# 13. Meaning of Different Values of K

The value of K tells us **which side the reaction prefers**.

### Case 1: Large K

$$
K \gg 1
$$

Example:

$$
K = 10^5
$$

Products dominate.

Reaction proceeds almost completely forward.

---

### Case 2: Small K

$$
K \ll 1
$$

Example:

$$
K = 10^{-5}
$$

Reactants dominate.

Very little product forms.

---

### Case 3: Moderate K

$$
K \approx 1
$$

Reactants and products are present in similar amounts.

---

# 14. Writing Equilibrium Expressions

For the reaction:

$$
aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD
$$

The equilibrium constant expression is:

$$
K_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}
$$

Important rules:

1. Coefficients become **powers**.
2. Only **gases and aqueous species** appear.
3. **Solids and liquids are omitted**.

---

Example:

$$
CaCO_3(s) \rightleftharpoons CaO(s) + CO_2(g)
$$

Equilibrium expression:

$$
K_c = [CO_2]
$$

---

Another example:

$$
AgCl(s) \rightleftharpoons Ag^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq)
$$

Expression:

$$
K = [Ag^+][Cl^-]
$$

---

# 15. Units of Equilibrium Constant

Units depend on the reaction.

Example:

$$
A \rightleftharpoons B
$$

Expression:

$$
K_c = \frac{[B]}{[A]}
$$

Units cancel → no unit.

---

Example:

$$
A + B \rightleftharpoons C
$$

Expression:

$$
K_c = \frac{[C]}{[A][B]}
$$

Units become:

$$
L \, mol^{-1}
$$

---

# 16. Equilibrium Constant in Terms of Pressure (Kp)

When reactions involve gases, equilibrium constant can also be written using **partial pressures**.

For reaction:

$$
aA(g) + bB(g) \rightleftharpoons cC(g) + dD(g)
$$

The equilibrium constant in terms of pressure is:

$$
K_p = \frac{(P_C)^c (P_D)^d}{(P_A)^a (P_B)^b}
$$

Where:

- $$P_A, P_B, P_C, P_D$$ = partial pressures of gases

---

# 17. Relation Between Kp and Kc

This relation is derived from the **ideal gas equation**.

Ideal gas equation:

$$
PV = nRT
$$

Rearranging:

$$
P = CRT
$$

Using this relationship, we obtain:

$$
K_p = K_c (RT)^{\Delta n}
$$

Where:

- $R$ = gas constant
- $T$ = temperature in Kelvin
- $\Delta n$ = change in number of moles of gases

---

Change in moles of gases:

$$
\Delta n = \text{moles of gaseous products} - \text{moles of gaseous reactants}
$$

---

Example:

$$
N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)
$$

Products moles:

$$
2
$$

Reactant moles:

$$
1 + 3 = 4
$$

So:

$$
\Delta n = 2 - 4 = -2
$$

Therefore:

$$
K_p = K_c (RT)^{-2}
$$

---

# 18. Special Cases

### Case 1: When $\Delta n = 0$

Example:

$$
H_2(g) + I_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2HI(g)
$$

Products moles:

$$
2
$$

Reactants moles:

$$
2
$$

So:

$$
\Delta n = 0
$$

Thus:

$$
K_p = K_c
$$

---

### Case 2: When $\Delta n > 0$

More gaseous moles on product side.

Then:

$$
K_p > K_c
$$

---

### Case 3: When $\Delta n < 0$

More gaseous moles on reactant side.

Then:

$$
K_p < K_c
$$

---

# 19. Reaction Quotient (Q)

Reaction quotient helps predict **which direction a reaction will proceed**.

Its formula is the same as equilibrium constant.

$$
Q = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}
$$

But concentrations may **not be equilibrium concentrations**.

---

# 20. Comparing Q and K

### If

$$
Q < K
$$

Reaction moves **forward** to produce more products.

---

### If

$$
Q > K
$$

Reaction moves **backward** to form more reactants.

---

### If

$$
Q = K
$$

The system is **already at equilibrium**.

---

# 21. ICE Tables (Very Important for JEE)

ICE stands for:

Initial  
Change  
Equilibrium  

Used to calculate equilibrium concentrations.

---

Example reaction:

$$
H_2 + I_2 \rightleftharpoons 2HI
$$

Initial concentrations:

$$
[H_2] = 1
$$

$$
[I_2] = 1
$$

$$
[HI] = 0
$$

---

Change during reaction:

$$
H_2 = -x
$$

$$
I_2 = -x
$$

$$
HI = +2x
$$

---

Equilibrium concentrations:

$$
[H_2] = 1 - x
$$

$$
[I_2] = 1 - x
$$

$$
[HI] = 2x
$$

Substitute in equilibrium constant expression:

$$
K_c = \frac{[HI]^2}{[H_2][I_2]}
$$

So:

$$
K_c = \frac{(2x)^2}{(1-x)(1-x)}
$$

Then solve for $$x$$.

---

# 22. Effect of Changing Equation on K

These questions appear frequently in exams.

---

### Reversing Reaction

Original reaction:

$$
A \rightleftharpoons B
$$

Reverse reaction:

$$
B \rightleftharpoons A
$$

New equilibrium constant:

$$
K' = \frac{1}{K}
$$

---

### Multiplying Reaction

If reaction is multiplied by $$n$$:

$$
A \rightleftharpoons B
$$

becomes

$$
nA \rightleftharpoons nB
$$

Then:

$$
K' = K^n
$$

---

### Dividing Reaction

If reaction is divided by $$n$$:

$$
K' = K^{1/n}
$$

---

# 23. Effect of Catalyst

A catalyst speeds up the reaction but **does not change equilibrium constant**.

Mathematically:

$$
K_{with\ catalyst} = K_{without\ catalyst}
$$

Catalyst only reduces **activation energy**.

---

# 24. Effect of Temperature

Equilibrium constant **depends only on temperature**.

If temperature changes:

$$
K_{new} \neq K_{old}
$$

This is the **only factor that changes K**.

---

# 25. Industrial Applications of Equilibrium

### Haber Process

Ammonia production:

$$
N_2 + 3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3
$$

Used in fertilizer manufacturing.

---

### Contact Process

Sulfuric acid production:

$$
2SO_2 + O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3
$$

---

### Methanol Synthesis

$$
CO + 2H_2 \rightleftharpoons CH_3OH
$$

Used for fuels and chemicals.

# 26. Quick Revision Sheet

Core formulas:

$$
K_c = \frac{[products]^{coeff}}{[reactants]^{coeff}}
$$

$$
K_p = K_c (RT)^{\Delta n}
$$

$$
\Delta n = n_{products} - n_{reactants}
$$

---

Reaction direction rules:

$$
Q < K \Rightarrow \text{Forward reaction}
$$

$$
Q > K \Rightarrow \text{Backward reaction}
$$

$$
Q = K \Rightarrow \text{Equilibrium}
$$

---

Important rules:

- Pure solids and liquids are excluded  
- Coefficients become powers  
- Only gases and aqueous species appear  
- Equilibrium constant depends only on temperature

# Problem 1 — Writing Equilibrium Expression

For the reaction:

$$
2NO(g) + O_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NO_2(g)
$$

Write the expression for the equilibrium constant \(K_c\).

---

## Solution

General form:

$$
aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD
$$

$$
K_c = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}
$$

Applying to the reaction:

$$
K_c = \frac{[NO_2]^2}{[NO]^2[O_2]}
$$

---

# Problem 2 — Ignoring Solids in Equilibrium

For the reaction:

$$
CaCO_3(s) \rightleftharpoons CaO(s) + CO_2(g)
$$

Write the equilibrium constant expression.

---

## Solution

Rule:

Pure solids and liquids **do not appear in equilibrium expressions**.

So the expression becomes:

$$
K_c = [CO_2]
$$

---

# Problem 3 — Finding \(K_c\)

For the reaction:

$$
H_2(g) + I_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2HI(g)
$$

At equilibrium:

$$
[H_2] = 0.5\,M
$$

$$
[I_2] = 0.5\,M
$$

$$
[HI] = 2.0\,M
$$

Find \(K_c\).

---

## Solution

Expression:

$$
K_c = \frac{[HI]^2}{[H_2][I_2]}
$$

Substitute values:

$$
K_c = \frac{(2.0)^2}{(0.5)(0.5)}
$$

$$
K_c = \frac{4}{0.25}
$$

$$
K_c = 16
$$

---

# Problem 4 — Finding Δn

For the reaction:

$$
N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)
$$

Find the value of \( \Delta n \).

---

## Solution

Formula:

$$
\Delta n = \text{moles of gaseous products} - \text{moles of gaseous reactants}
$$

Products:

$$
2
$$

Reactants:

$$
1 + 3 = 4
$$

Therefore:

$$
\Delta n = 2 - 4 = -2
$$

---

# Problem 5 — Relation Between \($K_p$\) and \($K_c$)

For the reaction:

$$
N_2 + 3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3
$$

If

$$
K_c = 0.5
$$

Temperature:

$$
T = 300K
$$

Find $(K_p)$.

Take:

$$
R = 0.0821
$$

---

## Solution

Formula:

$$
K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n}
$$

First calculate \( \Delta n \)

$$
\Delta n = 2 - 4 = -2
$$

Substitute values:

$$
K_p = 0.5(0.0821 \times 300)^{-2}
$$

First calculate:

$$
RT = 24.63
$$

So:

$$
K_p = 0.5(24.63)^{-2}
$$

$$
K_p = 0.5 \times \frac{1}{606.7}
$$

$$
K_p \approx 8.2 \times 10^{-4}
$$

# Problem 6 — Direction of Reaction Using Q

For reaction:

$$
H_2 + I_2 \rightleftharpoons 2HI
$$

Given:

$$
K_c = 50
$$

At some time:

$$
[H_2] = 1
$$

$$
[I_2] = 1
$$

$$
[HI] = 2
$$

Find direction of reaction.

---

## Solution

First calculate \(Q\).

Formula:

$$
Q = \frac{[HI]^2}{[H_2][I_2]}
$$

Substitute:

$$
Q = \frac{2^2}{1 \times 1}
$$

$$
Q = 4
$$

Now compare:

$$
Q < K
$$

$$
4 < 50
$$

Therefore:

Reaction moves **forward**.

More HI will form.

# Problem 7 — ICE Table Problem

Reaction:

$$
H_2 + I_2 \rightleftharpoons 2HI
$$

Initial concentrations:

$$
[H_2] = 1M
$$

$$
[I_2] = 1M
$$

$$
[HI] = 0
$$

At equilibrium:

$$
K_c = 4
$$

Find equilibrium concentrations.

---

## Solution

Let reaction proceed by \(x\).

### ICE Table

Initial:

$$
H_2 = 1
$$

$$
I_2 = 1
$$

$$
HI = 0
$$

---

Change:

$$
H_2 = -x
$$

$$
I_2 = -x
$$

$$
HI = +2x
$$

---

Equilibrium:

$$
H_2 = 1 - x
$$

$$
I_2 = 1 - x
$$

$$
HI = 2x
$$

---

Substitute in equilibrium expression:

$$
K_c = \frac{[HI]^2}{[H_2][I_2]}
$$

$$
4 = \frac{(2x)^2}{(1-x)(1-x)}
$$

Simplify:

$$
4 = \frac{4x^2}{(1-x)^2}
$$

Cancel 4:

$$
1 = \frac{x^2}{(1-x)^2}
$$

Take square root:

$$
x = 1-x
$$

So:

$$
x = 0.5
$$

---

### Final concentrations

$$
[H_2] = 0.5M
$$

$$
[I_2] = 0.5M
$$

$$
[HI] = 1M
$$

---
